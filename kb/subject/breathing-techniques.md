# Breathing Techniques Reference

## 1. Slow BP Breathing (6 breaths/min)

**Pattern:** 5s inhale → 5s exhale (no hold)
**Breaths per minute:** 6 (the "resonance frequency")
**Primary target:** Blood pressure reduction

### Mechanism
At 6 breaths/min, respiratory sinus arrhythmia (RSA) synchronises with Mayer waves (~0.1 Hz oscillations in blood pressure). This maximises baroreflex sensitivity — the body's primary BP regulation mechanism. The result is increased vagal tone and reduced sympathetic nervous system activity.

### Evidence
- Multiple RCTs show 4-10 mmHg systolic reduction with regular practice
- RESPeRATE (FDA-cleared device) is based on this principle
- Schein et al. (2001): Device-guided breathing at <10 breaths/min reduced SBP by 15.2 mmHg over 8 weeks. *Journal of Human Hypertension*, 15(4), 271-278.
- Elliott & Edmonson (2006): "Coherent breathing at 5 breaths/min with equal inhalation-to-exhalation ratio." *Coherent Breathing*, Allen Lane.

### Best for
Hypertension, daily practice, beginners

## 2. 4-7-8 Relaxing Breath

**Pattern:** 4s inhale → 7s hold → 8s exhale
**Breaths per minute:** ~3.2
**Primary target:** Anxiety, sleep, acute stress

### Mechanism
Extended exhale phase (8s) strongly activates the vagus nerve. The 7s hold increases CO2 tolerance and lung gas exchange time. Developed by Dr. Andrew Weil (University of Arizona) based on pranayama tradition.

### Evidence
- Vierra et al. (2022): 4-7-8 breathing reduced anxiety scores and improved HRV in college students. *International Journal of Psychophysiology*, 178, 42-49.
- Primarily evidence for anxiety and sleep, less direct BP trial data
- Dr. Weil recommends it as a "natural tranquiliser for the nervous system"

### Best for
Evening use, anxiety episodes, insomnia, stress

## 3. Box Breathing (4-4-4-4)

**Pattern:** 4s inhale → 4s hold → 4s exhale → 4s hold
**Breaths per minute:** ~3.75
**Primary target:** Focus, acute stress management

### Mechanism
Equal-phase breathing with breath holds activates both branches of the autonomic nervous system in a controlled manner. The predictable rhythm reduces cognitive load and activates the prefrontal cortex.

### Evidence
- Used by US Navy SEALs, Navy Special Warfare Command, and first responders
- Balban et al. (2023): Structured breathing practices (including cyclic sighing) enhanced mood and reduced physiological arousal more effectively than mindfulness meditation. *Cell Reports Medicine*, 4(1), 100895.
- Ma et al. (2017): Diaphragmatic breathing reduced cortisol and improved sustained attention. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 8, 874.

### Best for
Pre-meeting focus, acute stress, performance situations

---

## 4. Coherent Breathing (5-5)

**Pattern:** 5s inhale → 5s exhale (no hold)
**Breaths per minute:** 6
**Primary target:** HRV improvement

### Mechanism
Identical rate to Slow BP Breathing but specifically branded/studied for HRV maximisation. The key insight: at ~6 breaths/min, the respiratory cycle and heart's natural oscillations align, producing the largest possible HRV amplitude.

### Evidence
- Lehrer & Gevirtz (2014): HRV biofeedback (typically at ~6 breaths/min) improves baroreflex gain, which reduces BP. *Frontiers in Psychology*, 5, 756.
- Lin et al. (2019): HRV biofeedback training reduced BP in pre-hypertensive subjects. *Applied Psychophysiology and Biofeedback*, 44(3), 191-202.

### Best for
Long-term cardiovascular health, daily practice, measurable HRV improvement

---

## 5. IMST (Inspiratory Muscle Strength Training)

**Pattern:** Sharp/forceful inhale 5s → 2s hold → slow exhale 5s
**Breaths per minute:** ~5
**Primary target:** Blood pressure reduction (strongest evidence)

### Mechanism
Forceful inhalation against resistance (or simulated resistance) strengthens the diaphragm and inspiratory muscles. This improves vascular endothelial function, increases nitric oxide bioavailability, and reduces oxidative stress — all of which lower BP independent of the calming effects of slow breathing.

### Evidence
- **Craighead et al. (2021)**: 30 breaths/day at 75% max inspiratory pressure for 6 weeks. Result: **SBP reduced by 9 mmHg** — comparable to first-line antihypertensive drugs. Also improved endothelial function by 45%. *Journal of the American Heart Association*, 10(13), e020980.
- **Craighead et al. (2023)**: Benefits persisted for 6 weeks after stopping training. *Journal of Applied Physiology*.
- Only 5 minutes per day required (lowest time commitment of any intervention)
- University of Colorado Boulder research

### Important notes
- Original studies used a physical resistance device (POWERbreathe)
- App-based version without resistance device is a simplified adaptation
- The breathing pattern alone provides some benefit but less than with resistance
- Full IMST requires a certified device (~$30-60)

### Best for
People who want maximum BP reduction in minimum time, those willing to use a device

## Technique Selection Guide

| Goal | Best technique | Why |
|------|---------------|-----|
| Lower BP (daily) | Slow BP or Coherent | Most evidence, easiest habit |
| Lower BP (max effect) | IMST (with device) | 9 mmHg reduction in trials |
| Acute anxiety | 4-7-8 | Fast-acting vagal activation |
| Pre-meeting focus | Box | Military-proven for performance |
| Improve HRV | Coherent | Maximises HRV amplitude |
| Sleep | 4-7-8 | "Natural tranquiliser" |
| Beginner | Slow BP | Simple, no holds, no counting |

## Session Duration Recommendations

| Level | Duration | Frequency |
|-------|----------|-----------|
| Getting started | 3-5 min | 1x/day |
| Building habit | 5-10 min | 1x/day |
| Therapeutic dose | 10-15 min | 1-2x/day |
| IMST protocol | 5 min (30 breaths) | 1x/day |

**Key evidence:** Most clinical trials used 15 min/day for 8+ weeks. Benefits are cumulative and dose-dependent.
